About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Computer Science at Northeastern University's Khoury College of Computer Sciences in Boston, Massachusetts. I am working under the supervision of Prof. Casper Harteveld. My research lies at the intersection of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and Digital Health, with a strong emphasis on designing user-centered systems that foster ethical and empathetic digital experiences. Currently, my work focuses on designing and evaluating female-oriented health applications. I have experience in qualitative and quantitative research methods, usability testing, UX design, and participatory and transdisciplinary research.

Before joining Northeastern University, I worked as a Teaching Assistant at King Saud University in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, where I earned a MSc in Information Systems and a BSc in Information Technology. Additionally, I have professional experience as a Systems Analyst in the Clinical Information Management Department at the National Guard Health Affairs in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia.

Latest Research

"An Ad Posing as Medical Advice": User Accounts of Dark UX in FemTech mHealth Apps

What if the app that knows your most intimate details used them against you? That is the uncomfortable reality we explore in this work. We analyze user accounts of questionable design practices in the intimate context of FemTech (female technology). Drawing from reviews of apps for menstruation, fertility, pregnancy, and menopause, we highlight experiences where design felt coercive, exploitative, or outright harmful through design patterns— such as data traps, unexpected paywalls at critical moments, and age-related consent gaps.

*Ghada Alsebayel, Giovanni M Troiano, Johanna Gunawan, Herman Saksono, and Casper Harteveld. 2025. “An Ad Posing as Medical Advice”: User Accounts of Dark UX in FemTech mHealth Apps. Proc. ACM Hum.-Comput. Interact. 9, 5, Article MHCI003 (September 2025), 34 pages.
Presented in MobileHCI 2025, Sharm El Sheikh, Egypt.

"I Believe the Baby in the Picture is My Baby": UX with Commercial Pregnancy Apps

This study explores how users experience and interact with commercial pregnancy-tracking apps. Through thematic analysis of user reviews, we outline avenues to improve the design of pregnancy technologies by authentically embodying diverse pregnancy experiences and facilitating meaningful participation in shared and integrated pregnancy experiences.

ACM DL   Featured

Honorable Mention Award in MobileHCI 2024 — Melbourne, Australia.

"Guilty of Talking Too Much": How Psychotherapists Gamify Therapy

This study explores how psychotherapists create digitally gamified psychotherapies. The prototypes created by psychotherapists were analyzed from a game design and psychotherapy perspective, presenting insights into strategies and approaches for digitally gamifying therapy and identifying challenges and opportunities for the future of gamified psychotherapy.

ACM DL    Presentation

Elina Tochilnikova, Amrit Patnaik, *Ghada Alsebayel, Uttkarsh Narayan, Andrew Coeytaux, Valeria Ramdin, Miso Kim, and Casper Harteveld. 2022. “Guilty of Talking Too Much”: How Psychotherapists Gamify Therapy. In Proceedings of the 2022 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI '22). Association for Computing Machinery, New York, NY, USA, Article 445, 1–17.

ArticuMotion: Towards Assessing Motor Speech Disorders via Gamification

ArticuMotion is an initiative that explores the use of games and gamification in speech-language assessment for preschool children (ages 3-5). Our goal is to support Speech-Language Pathologists in understanding how the speech movements of young children with speech delays or disorders differ from those with typical speech development, which may enable early detection and intervention.

Community Service

Co-designed and co-organized an annual Computer Science (CS) summer school for girls at King Saud University, held from 2016 to 2018. The program introduced high school students to foundational concepts in artificial intelligence, robotics, programming, and game development, aiming to inspire and encourage more young women to pursue careers in STEM. The initiative was well-received and featured in local media in Saudi Arabia. You can find participants hashtag engagement in #2016, #2017, #2018.
